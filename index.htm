REAL OR FAKE?:

Lisa Caprelli states the reason most companies start to advertise through social media because it allows them to become active, builds the brand and is key for keeping in connection with the customers. The easiest way of noticing a scam would be the opposite of this; not constantly active, creating a fake brand online instead of building on a real one in real life and once getting money of customers disappearing without any connection leading to them. Finding out whether a brand is real or not online can simply be down by a few minutes of research.

INTRODUCTION

In 2017, on average a human being spent around 135 minutes per day on social media, scrolling through images of friends, humorous tweets/statuses, reacting to posts and being bombarded by marketing campaigns. Advertising is seen everywhere, all the time, but how has this new technology age been able to grab a consumer’s attention through the screen? With the large scale of the internet, false advertisement is becoming easier to get away with; currently 2019 only a month and half into the year Scamwatch states that the public has lost nearly $5,000,000 in scams online already this year. Fighting a battle to not click on a holiday to New York for 20 pounds or claiming a prize for IPad after using a Facebook game can be hard; so how do we stop ourselves and what can we look out for.

5 COMMON INSTAGRAM SCAMS EVERYONE FALLS FOR:

As you can see from the on the left two images @tescofood is a real business account, this can be known by the verification tick from Instagram. The blue verification tick means Instagram has confirmed that an account is the authentic presence of the public figure, celebrity or global brand it represents. This is an easy way to know whether businesses are real or fake; however not all real businesses have them, just larger organisations. As Jeffrey K.Rohrs states in Audience: Marketing in the Age of Subscribers, fans and following; ‘Getting an audience is HARD. Sustaining an audience is Hard. It demands consistency of thought, of purpose and of action over a long period of time’. Tesco’s Instagram also has a large number of followers, showing the user that many other users have followed and trusted this account; which would reassure the user as in real life if someone else recommends a product to you, you’re more likely to buy it. Furthermore, Tesco has a large amount of feed posts (2190), this shows that they have been on Instagram for a long period of time; a fake company trying to scam you would be able to stay active for a long period of time in order to avoid being caught.

There are many differences between the two accounts in the photos above; looking at the @kululaairways account you can clearly see that it does not have the same presence as @tescofood. Firstly, it has no blue verification tick, which means Instagram has not verified that this is a real company unlike Tesco. With only 27 followers, this also reassures this account is fake as they are offering an incredible free holiday for the first 25,000 followers; if the account was real there would be a lot more attraction. With only one post on its feed, the account is very inactive, meaning that it does not have to reply to all the scammed users wanting a free holiday. There is 0 proof anywhere of the deal being real with 0 photos of the holiday or any person you can see behind the brand. Tesco has many professional photos which shows the user what they our buying adding trust.
